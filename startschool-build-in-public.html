01 Why building in public works

You don't need connections.
You need visibility.

Most founders wait until they have something perfect to show. Building in public flips that. The process is the product — and the people watching become your first customers, advisors, and advocates.

Your audience self-selects

The people who follow your journey already believe in what you're doing. When you're ready to sell, they've been watching you solve the problem for months.

Feedback before you build

Share the idea, the struggle, the pivot. People will tell you what's wrong before you spend six months building the wrong thing. It's free market research.

Compound interest on trust

Each post is a small deposit. After 90 days of showing up, you've built something no ad budget can buy — a community that trusts you because they watched you earn it.

AI means one person can build what used to take twenty. Building in public means the right twenty people find you while you're building it.

02 What to share

The content is already
happening to you.

You don't need to create content. You need to narrate what's already happening. Here's a framework — three types of posts that work at any stage.

Type
What it looks like
Why it works
Progress
What happened
"Week 3 building [product]. Got our first 10 users. Here's what surprised us..."
People root for momentum. Numbers — even small ones — signal real traction.
Learning
What you discovered
"We thought our customer was X. After 20 interviews, it's actually Y. Here's what changed."
Vulnerability + insight = the most shareable content. Everyone's made the same mistake.
Decision
What you chose
"We had to choose between X and Y. We picked Y because... What would you have done?"
Invites engagement. Makes followers feel part of your journey, not just observers.
Behind scenes
How it's made
"Here's the exact AI prompt I use to write customer emails. Saves me 2 hrs/week."
Generosity builds authority faster than showing off. Give the good stuff away.

What NOT to share

❌ Your revenue before you have any

❌ Vague inspiration ("Working on something big 👀")

❌ Competitor comparisons / negativity

❌ Customers' private feedback without permission

❌ Anything you'd regret in a fundraising meeting

❌ Posting once then going silent for 3 weeks

03 How to write it

The anatomy of a post
people actually read.

Most founder posts fail in the first line. Here's the structure that works — with real examples at each stage.

The formula

1
Hook — one line that stops the scroll. A number, a confession, or a counterintuitive truth.
2
Context — 1–2 sentences: who you are, what you're building, why this moment matters.
3
The thing — the actual update, insight, or decision. Be specific. Numbers help.
4
What you learned — the takeaway. Make it portable so others can apply it to their situation.
5
Ask or next — a question to invite replies, or what's coming next week.

05 Common mistakes

Why most founders
quit in week three.

Building in public fails for predictable reasons. Here are the six most common — and what to do instead.

❌
Waiting until you have something to show

The point is to show the process. "I have no product yet" is not a reason to wait — it's the content. Document the problem you're solving and why you care about it.

❌
Posting metrics without context

"We hit $1K MRR" means nothing without the story. Why did it take so long? What finally worked? The number is the headline — the lesson is the content.

✓
Consistency beats quality every time

A mediocre post every week for a year outperforms a perfect post every month. The algorithm rewards consistency. More importantly — your audience remembers you exist.

❌
Only sharing wins

Your wins make people happy. Your failures make people trust you. The founder who admits they mispriced their product and what they did about it gets 5x the engagement.

✓
Reply to every comment, especially early on

Your first 50 followers are gold. Reply to everyone. Ask them questions. Turn observers into participants — they'll become your most loyal advocates when you have something to sell.

❌
Treating it like broadcasting

Build in public is a conversation, not a press release. Ask questions. Post polls. Comment on other founders' posts. The network you build matters as much as the content.

06 Your 30-day plan

Start tomorrow.
Not when you're ready.

Here's a week-by-week plan for your first month. No followers required. No product required. Just an honest account of what you're building and why.

1
Week 1 — Day 1–7
Introduce yourself and the problem

Write one post: who you are, what problem you're solving, and why you care about it. Post it. Don't overthink it. This is your origin story — people will reference it later. Then spend the rest of the week commenting on 5 posts a day from founders you respect.

2
Week 2 — Day 8–14
Document your customer research

Post what you're learning from customer conversations. "I interviewed 8 people this week. Here's the one thing they all said that surprised me." Share the insight, not just the fact that you did interviews.

3
Week 3 — Day 15–21
Share a decision you had to make

What was the hardest choice you made this week? Pricing, positioning, who to build for first? Walk through the decision. Ask your audience what they would have done. Engagement will be 3x a regular post.

4
Week 4 — Day 22–30
Post your first monthly recap

What happened in month one? What did you expect vs. what actually happened? Metrics, learnings, what's next. This is your most important post — it anchors your credibility and gives new followers a reason to follow your next chapter.

The only metric that matters in month one

Not impressions. Not followers. Did a stranger DM you because of something you posted? That's one potential customer. That's the whole point.
